AXIS 1.3: TOPOLOGICAL DEFECT THEORY & HIGH-DIMENSIONAL BURGERS VECTORS
================================================================================

1. THE HIGHER-DIMENSIONAL VOLTERRA PROCESS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
In a standard periodic crystal, a dislocation line is created by cutting the 
lattice along a plane, sliding the faces past each other by a discrete lattice 
translation vector (the Burgers vector, b), and rejoining them. Because the 
lattice is periodic, the atomic structure across the cut heals perfectly, 
leaving a localized line defect.

In an icosahedral quasicrystal, this standard 3D operation fails because a 
translation in physical space (E_||) does not match the long-range aperiodic 
order. The Volterra process must be executed in the higher-dimensional parent 
lattice (Z⁶). The generalized higher-dimensional Burgers vector (B) must be 
defined as a 6-component tensor:

  B = (b_||, b_⊥)

Where:
  - b_||: The physical component of the Burgers vector manifested in E_||. It 
          dictates the long-range elastic strain field and couples to phonons.
  - b_⊥: The phason component of the Burgers vector manifested in E_⊥. It 
          dictates the localized structural mismatch and couples to phasons.

2. THE PHASON WALL AND THE GEOMETRIC RETARDING FORCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When an external mechanical shear stress forces a dislocation line to glide 
through the aperiodic material, the line translates by the physical vector b_||. 
However, because the atomic environment does not repeat periodically, this 
motion systematically violates the higher-dimensional selection rule:

  x_⊥ ∈ [0, W)

As the dislocation line cuts through the structure, it drags the selection window 
out of alignment, leaving a trailing two-dimensional defect sheet in its wake. 
This sheet is a PHASON WALL (or phason fault). Across this wall, the local atomic 
bonds are heavily distorted, and the strict φ-proportional sequencing of Long 
and Short intervals is broken.

The creation of a phason wall requires a distinct input of energy per unit area, 
defined as the phason fault energy (γ_phason). Consequently, as the dislocation 
line attempts to glide, it experiences a continuous, geometric retarding stress 
(σ_retarding) proportional to the fault energy:

  σ_retarding = γ_phason / |b_|||

3. APERIODIC PINNING VS. PERIODIC PEIERLS-NABARRO STRESS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
In a standard periodic crystal, a dislocation only experiences the localized 
Peierls-Nabarro stress—a periodic energy barrier that varies sinusoidally as the 
core moves from one symmetric lattice site to the next. The lattice resistance 
is completely local and self-canceling once the barrier is crossed.

In a quasicrystal, the phason wall acts as an unyielding geometric brake. The 
resistance does not cancel out; it accumulates because the trailing phason wall 
grows longer with every step the dislocation takes. The dislocation line is 
effectively pinned by its own trail of structural mismatches. 

At low temperatures (T -> 0), plastic deformation via dislocation glide is 
virtually locked out, rendering the material exceptionally hard but brittle. 
Plasticity can only be unlocked at high temperatures, where thermal energy 
allows the trailing phason wall to diffuse and heal via stochastically activated 
phason flips, allowing the cutting toolpath to move forward.
